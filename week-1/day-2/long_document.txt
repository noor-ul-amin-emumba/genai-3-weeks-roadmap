ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE IN CLIMATE CHANGE MITIGATION AND ADAPTATION
A Comprehensive Technical and Policy Review
Report No. ENV-2024-117 | Global Institute for Climate Technology (GICT)
Published: September 2024 | Authors: Dr. Priya Nair, Dr. Samuel Osei, Dr. Lena Brandt

================================================================================
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

Artificial intelligence (AI) has emerged as one of the most transformative tools
available to climate scientists, policymakers, and engineers working on climate
change mitigation and adaptation. This report reviews the breadth of AI
applications across five major domains: climate modelling and prediction, renewable
energy optimization, carbon monitoring and capture, climate-related financial risk,
and policy decision support.

Key findings include:

  - AI-enhanced climate models have reduced 10-year precipitation forecast errors
    by 34% compared with traditional numerical weather prediction (NWP) models
    (Zhang et al., 2023, Nature Climate Change).
  - Machine-learning-optimized wind-farm layouts have demonstrated 11–18% gains in
    annual energy yield over conventional design heuristics, based on a meta-analysis
    of 47 commercial wind projects in Europe (Osei & Lindqvist, 2024).
  - Satellite-based AI carbon-flux models now detect deforestation events within
    72 hours of occurrence at 10-metre spatial resolution, enabling near-real-time
    REDD+ verification (Nair et al., 2023, Remote Sensing of Environment).
  - Climate-risk AI tools deployed by 12 central banks collectively cover assets
    worth $14.7 trillion, though model uncertainty disclosure remains inconsistent.

The report concludes that while AI offers substantial promise, deployment at scale
requires attention to data quality, computational energy costs, interpretability,
and equity considerations in Global South contexts.

================================================================================
CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

1.1 Background and Motivation

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Sixth Assessment Report (2021)
stated with unequivocal confidence that human influence has warmed the climate at
an unprecedented rate. Average global surface temperatures in 2023 reached 1.45°C
above the 1850–1900 pre-industrial baseline, the highest since systematic records
began (WMO Global Climate Report, 2024). Limiting warming to 1.5°C requires cutting
global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by approximately 43% by 2030 and reaching net
zero by 2050 (IPCC AR6, Chapter 4).

Against this backdrop, artificial intelligence has attracted intense interest as a
force multiplier for climate action. AI systems excel at identifying patterns in
high-dimensional data—a property directly applicable to climate science, which
generates petabytes of satellite, sensor, and model-output data annually.

1.2 Scope of This Report

This report covers peer-reviewed literature and grey-literature reports published
between January 2019 and August 2024. It is structured around five application
domains: (1) climate modelling and prediction, (2) renewable energy, (3) carbon
monitoring, (4) financial risk, and (5) policy support. Each chapter surveys the
state of the technology, quantifies demonstrated impact where data are available,
and identifies key barriers to wider adoption.

1.3 Terminology

  - Machine Learning (ML): statistical techniques that allow systems to learn from
    data without being explicitly programmed for each task.
  - Deep Learning (DL): a subclass of ML using multi-layer neural networks,
    particularly effective for image, time-series, and natural-language data.
  - Large Language Models (LLMs): transformer-based models trained on vast text
    corpora, now increasingly used for literature synthesis and policy drafting.
  - Digital Twin: a real-time computational replica of a physical system (e.g., a
    power grid or an estuary) used for scenario simulation.

================================================================================
CHAPTER 2: AI IN CLIMATE MODELLING AND PREDICTION
================================================================================

2.1 Limitations of Traditional Numerical Weather Prediction

Numerical weather prediction (NWP) systems such as the ECMWF's Integrated
Forecasting System (IFS) discretize the atmosphere, ocean, and land surface into
grid cells and integrate differential equations forward in time. These models are
scientifically mature and operationally robust, but carry high computational costs:
a single global 10-day forecast with IFS consumes roughly 1,200 CPU-core hours.
They also struggle to capture sub-grid-scale processes (e.g., convective
precipitation) without expensive parameterisation schemes.

2.2 Data-Driven Weather Forecasting

A new generation of purely data-driven models has demonstrated forecast skill
comparable to—and sometimes exceeding—operational NWP at a fraction of the
computational cost.

  - Pangu-Weather (Huawei, 2023): trained on 39 years of ERA5 reanalysis data;
    produces 1-week global forecasts in under 1 second on a single GPU versus
    ~1,200 CPU-core hours for IFS. Achieved lower root-mean-square error (RMSE)
    on 24/48/72-hour 500 hPa geopotential height than IFS in head-to-head
    evaluation on 2018 test data (Bi et al., 2023, Nature).
  - GraphCast (Google DeepMind, 2023): graph neural network architecture; matched
    or exceeded IFS on 90.3% of 1,380 atmospheric variables at forecast leads of
    1–10 days (Lam et al., 2023, Science).
  - FourCastNet (NVIDIA, 2022): convolutional architecture; 45,000× faster than
    IFS for equivalent-resolution forecasts; used operationally for hurricane track
    prediction by NOAA as of Q3 2023.

2.3 Climate Projection Emulators

Beyond short-range forecasting, ML emulators accelerate long-range climate
projections. Traditional Earth system models (ESMs) require weeks of supercomputer
time to produce century-scale runs. Neural emulators trained on ESM output can
reproduce regional temperature and precipitation distributions at 1,000–10,000×
speed, enabling large ensemble uncertainty quantification that is otherwise
computationally prohibitive.

ClimateBench (Watson-Parris et al., 2022, Journal of Advances in Modeling Earth
Systems) established a benchmark suite for emulator evaluation, reporting that
gradient-boosted regression trees achieved global mean temperature (GMT) emulation
with RMSE < 0.05°C relative to full ESM runs across the CMIP6 scenario ensemble.

2.4 Extreme Event Detection and Attribution

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) trained on reanalysis datasets have been
applied to pattern-recognition tasks in climate data—most notably identifying
atmospheric rivers, tropical cyclone tracks, and blocking anticyclones at scales and
speeds impossible with manual analysis.

The ClimateNet benchmark (Kashinath et al., 2021, Geoscientific Model Development)
provided labelled segmentation data for 15 years of climate model output; CNN models
trained on this benchmark achieved intersection-over-union (IoU) scores of 0.76 for
atmospheric river detection, matching expert human labellers.

In event attribution—determining how much climate change altered the probability of
a specific extreme event—ML accelerates the generation of large counterfactual
ensembles, reducing the time for a full attribution study from ~6 months to ~72 hours
(Diffenbaugh & Barnes, 2023, Nature Climate Change).

CHAPTER 3: AI IN RENEWABLE ENERGY
================================================================================

3.1 Solar Power Forecasting

Solar irradiance forecasting is critical for grid stability because PV generation
is inherently variable. Deep-learning models ingesting satellite imagery, weather
model outputs, and historical generation data have consistently outperformed
persistence and NWP-based benchmarks.

  - Benchmark study (Nie et al., 2022, Applied Energy): LSTM + attention models
    reduced day-ahead global horizontal irradiance (GHI) forecasting error (RMSE)
    by 22.4% versus NWP alone across 12 sites in Germany.
  - SolarEdge production deployment: proprietary ML models reduced curtailment at
    partnered utility-scale farms by 8.3% in 2023, equivalent to ~430 GWh of
    additional generation across the fleet.

3.2 Wind Energy Optimization

3.2.1 Wake Modelling and Farm Layout

Wind turbine wakes—regions of reduced wind speed and increased turbulence behind
operating turbines—cost European wind farms an estimated €1.2 billion annually in
lost energy (WindEurope, 2023). Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) have been
used to model wake interactions at farm scale with 97% accuracy compared to
high-fidelity computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations, while running in
milliseconds versus hours.

A meta-analysis of 47 European commercial wind projects using ML-optimized layouts
(Osei & Lindqvist, 2024, Wind Energy) found annual energy production (AEP) gains of
11–18% versus conventional design heuristics. The median gain was 14.2%, with higher
gains observed in complex terrain.

3.2.2 Predictive Maintenance

Turbine downtime costs approximately €45,000–€65,000 per event (IRENA, 2023).
ML-based predictive maintenance using SCADA sensor streams has reduced unplanned
downtime by 25–40% in pilot deployments (Tautz-Weinert & Watson, 2023, Renewable
and Sustainable Energy Reviews). Random forests and gradient-boosted trees remain
the most widely adopted algorithms due to their interpretability and robustness on
tabular sensor data.

3.3 Grid Management and Demand Forecasting

Balancing electricity supply and demand in real time is increasingly complex as
variable renewables reach high penetrations. AI has been applied to:

  - Short-term load forecasting: hybrid CNN-LSTM models achieve <1.5% mean absolute
    percentage error (MAPE) on 24-hour ahead demand forecasts for the UK national
    grid (National Grid ESO, 2023 Annual Review).
  - Optimal dispatch: deep reinforcement learning agents trained in simulation have
    demonstrated 3–7% cost reductions versus rule-based dispatch in grid-scale
    battery storage operations (Cao et al., 2020, IEEE Transactions on Smart Grid).
  - Congestion management: graph neural networks modelling power-flow topology
    identified 18% more congestion events in advance, allowing pre-emptive
    rescheduling and avoiding £210 million in estimated constraint costs in a 2023
    UK pilot (National Grid ESO, 2023).

CHAPTER 4: AI IN CARBON MONITORING, REPORTING AND VERIFICATION (MRV)
================================================================================

4.1 Deforestation and Land-Use Change Detection

Tropical deforestation accounts for approximately 10% of annual global GHG
emissions. Timely detection is essential for REDD+ scheme enforcement and
compliance carbon markets.

The Global Forest Watch AI platform (Hansen et al., 2013, updated 2023) processes
daily Sentinel-2 and Landsat imagery using multi-temporal CNNs:

  - Detection latency: mean 68 hours from deforestation event to alert issuance
    (benchmark target < 72 hours achieved since Q2 2022).
  - False positive rate: 4.1% (down from 11.3% in 2019 due to training data
    augmentation and semi-supervised learning techniques).
  - Spatial resolution: 10 m for Sentinel-2-based alerts, 30 m for Landsat-based.

Nair et al. (2023, Remote Sensing of Environment) demonstrated that combining SAR
(Synthetic Aperture Radar) data with optical imagery using multi-modal fusion CNNs
reduced cloud-cover-induced detection gaps from 31% of alerts to 7% in the Congo
Basin during the wet season.

4.2 Methane Emission Detection

Methane (CH4) is a potent GHG with a 100-year global warming potential 27–30× that
of CO2. Point-source CH4 emissions from oil-and-gas infrastructure are notoriously
under-reported in national inventories.

MethaneSAT (Environmental Defense Fund, launched March 2024) uses onboard ML
inference to identify super-emitter plumes from hyperspectral satellite imagery in
near-real-time. Early results suggest that reported emissions from the Permian Basin
(USA) were underestimated by a factor of 1.8–2.4× relative to satellite-detected
levels (EDF Technical Report, April 2024).

4.3 Ocean Carbon Flux

The ocean absorbs approximately 26% of anthropogenic CO2 annually. Quantifying this
flux accurately is essential for global carbon budgets. Traditional ship-based
measurements are sparse. ML models trained on biogeochemical Argo float data and
satellite sea-surface temperature and colour have been used to construct global
monthly pCO2 fields at 1° resolution:

  - Friedlingstein et al. (2023, Earth System Science Data) report that ML-based
    gap-filling reduced the uncertainty range of the ocean carbon sink estimate from
    ±0.6 GtC/yr to ±0.4 GtC/yr, a 33% improvement.

CHAPTER 5: AI IN CLIMATE FINANCE AND RISK
================================================================================

5.1 Physical Risk Assessment

Climate physical risks—the economic impacts of extreme weather events and long-term
shifts in climate—pose systemic threats to financial stability. Regulatory bodies
including the Bank of England, ECB, and Federal Reserve now require financial
institutions to conduct climate scenario analysis.

AI applications in physical risk assessment include:

  - Flood risk modelling: NN-based hydraulic emulators produce 1-in-100-year
    coastal flood extents at building-level resolution in under 10 seconds
    vs. ~6 hours for full hydrodynamic models (Wing et al., 2022, Nature
    Communications). JPMorgan Chase deployed such a system in 2023 covering
    $620 billion in real-estate-secured loans.
  - Wildfire spread prediction: ensemble ML models incorporating fuel moisture,
    topography, and wind fields achieve 85% accuracy on 6-hour spread forecasts;
    deployed by Pacific Gas & Electric for pre-emptive de-energisation decisions.
  - Heatwave mortality forecasting: gradient boosting models combining
    climate projections with population vulnerability indices estimate
    excess deaths attributable to climatically unusual heat events with
    cross-validated R² = 0.87 (Chen et al., 2023, Lancet Planetary Health).

5.2 Transition Risk and Carbon Pricing

Transition risk—the economic dislocation from decarbonisation policies—is harder
to model because it depends on policy trajectories as well as physical science.
NLP models applied to earnings-call transcripts and regulatory filings
can extract forward-looking emissions-related disclosures and measure corporate
climate ambition at scale (Kölbel et al., 2022, Review of Finance). The TCFD-aligned
AI screening tools used by 12 central banks collectively cover assets worth
$14.7 trillion as of 2024.

5.3 Limitations and Concerns

  - Data vintage: many physical risk models are trained on 20th-century loss data
    that may not reflect 21st-century climate regimes.
  - Model opacity: black-box ML risk scores create accountability challenges for
    regulators and litigation exposure for financial institutions.
  - Double-counting: in portfolio aggregation, spatially correlated assets may
    share underlying climate risk drivers that naive aggregation treats as
    independent.

CHAPTER 6: CHALLENGES AND LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

6.1 Data Quality and Coverage Gaps

AI climate models are only as good as their training data. Key gaps include:

  - Sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia are severely under-represented in
    high-resolution observed climate datasets relative to Europe and North America.
  - Historical emissions inventories contain systematic under-reporting biases,
    particularly for agriculture, waste, and informal industrial sectors.
  - Ground-truth labels for supervised learning tasks (e.g., land-cover, poverty,
    infrastructure damage) are expensive to produce at scale in data-poor regions.

6.2 Computational Energy and Carbon Cost

Training large climate AI models carries its own carbon footprint. Training a single
100-billion-parameter foundation model is estimated to emit 590 tCO2e (Patterson et
al., 2022, Communications of the ACM). While inference is cheaper, running planetary-
scale remote sensing pipelines continuously requires substantial energy. Lifecycle
analysis for AI climate applications remains an underdeveloped research area.

6.3 Interpretability and Trust

Many ML models used in high-stakes climate decisions (e.g., wildfire evacuation
orders, climate-adjusted mortgage pricing) are not interpretable to end-users. This
limits trust and adoption among domain scientists and policymakers who are accustomed
to physical-mechanistic reasoning.

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) and LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic
Explanations) have been applied to post-hoc attribution in climate risk models, but
their computational cost at scale remains prohibitive and they do not provide
mechanistic understanding.

6.4 Equity and Access

Climate AI tools are primarily developed by well-resourced research institutions and
commercial firms in the Global North. Climate-vulnerable nations, which are
disproportionately in the Global South and carry less historical responsibility for
emissions, face barriers to accessing and adapting these tools due to:

  - Cost of compute infrastructure
  - Lack of locally relevant training data
  - Shortage of AI/ML expertise
  - Algorithmic outputs that reflect Global North socioeconomic assumptions

The IPCC AR6 Working Group II (Impacts, Adaptation and Vulnerability) flagged this
equity gap as a high-confidence finding, noting that unmediated deployment of AI
adaptation tools risks reinforcing existing inequalities.

6.5 Model Reliability Under Distribution Shift

Climate change is by definition a progressive shift in distributional properties of
the climate system. ML models trained on historical data may degrade in performance
as the distribution they were trained on drifts over time. This out-of-distribution
(OOD) degradation is a central open research challenge. While domain-adaptation
techniques and physics-informed constraints can mitigate OOD failures, no general
solution exists.

CHAPTER 7: FUTURE DIRECTIONS
================================================================================

7.1 Foundation Models for Climate Science

The success of large language models (e.g., GPT-4, Claude) and vision foundation
models (e.g., SAM, DINOv2) has inspired efforts to build analogous foundation models
pre-trained on diverse climate and Earth-observation data. Projects include:

  - ClimaX (Nguyen et al., 2023, ICML): a transformer pre-trained on 5 CMIP6 model
    ensembles and ERA5, achieving top-1 performance on six ClimateBench tasks via
    fine-tuning, demonstrating strong transfer learning to new climate variables
    and regions.
  - Earth-2 (NVIDIA, 2024): an initiative combining foundation modelling with
    digital-twin infrastructure, targeting metre-scale urban climate projections.

7.2 Active Learning and Human-in-the-Loop Systems

Given the cost of expert labelling, active learning—where models identify the most
informative unlabelled samples to query—offers a pathway to efficient data
collection in underserved regions. Early results show 60–70% label reduction for
equivalent model performance in deforestation detection tasks (Rolf et al., 2021).

7.3 Federated and Privacy-Preserving Learning

Climate and climate-risk data often carry commercial or national-security
sensitivities that prevent centralised aggregation. Federated learning, where model
weights rather than raw data are shared, is being explored for cross-border crop
yield modelling and flood risk pooling.

7.4 Integration with Policy Processes

LLMs are increasingly used to synthesise scientific literature for IPCC-style
assessments, draft national adaptation plans, and translate technical climate
projections into accessible policy briefs. Key risks include hallucination of
citations and exaggeration of certainty, reinforcing the need for human oversight.

================================================================================
CONCLUSION
================================================================================

Artificial intelligence offers genuine and substantial capability to accelerate
climate change mitigation and adaptation across the full value chain—from more
accurate climate projections, through renewable energy optimisation, to carbon
monitoring and climate-risk finance. The evidence base, while still maturing, shows
consistent improvements over conventional methods in controlled evaluations.

However, the deployment of AI for climate action is not a technocratic silver bullet.
The challenges outlined in Chapter 6—data gaps, computational costs, interpretability
deficits, equity concerns, and distribution shift—are substantive and require
coordinated investment in research infrastructure, governance frameworks, and
capacity building in climate-vulnerable nations.

The authors recommend:

  1. Mandatory lifecycle carbon accounting for large-scale climate AI deployments.
  2. Open-access repositories for climate AI training datasets, with priority given
     to Global South coverage.
  3. Interpretability requirements for AI models used in public-interest climate
     decisions (analogous to financial model risk management frameworks).
  4. International knowledge-transfer programmes to build AI/ML capacity in
     climate-vulnerable nations.
  5. Continued investment in hybrid physics-ML approaches that combine data-driven
     flexibility with mechanistic constraints, improving robustness under
     distribution shift.
